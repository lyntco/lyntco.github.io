## What level are you?

For a long time I thought my work was at the "Intermediate" level. 

After a bunch of analysis into what there is to learn, I now know I am a beginner still. 

But I think the word "beginner" does not properly encapsulate someone who has drawn after spending so many years on something so I will rename it to something else and add a different beginner tier to cover those who are just starting, and I have aligned them with the four stages of competence. Where I am now I would call myself in between a Casual or a Trainee.
A note here is that at any of these levels there may be teachers - their competency in drawing/painting may not line up with their competency to teach. Additionally here the word "Incompetence" is used. This word is sometimes used as an insulting word - but it is not here. It is meant to describe your proficiency in this particular skill area. It is not meant to demean or diminish your passion or effort. The most important thing here is that if you are currently incompetent, you can gain competence.


### Beginners/Casuals - Unconscious Incompetence
> “I don’t know what I don’t know.”
Don't spend a lot of time drawing - think less than once a month
Probably have not made money from their work
Don't know the rules/fundamentals
Draws in symbols
Does not know about intention
Can't copy from reference well
No idea of consistency
Does not have a developed style


### Trainee/Inexperienced - Conscious Incompetence
> “I know what I don’t know.”
May draw every week OR on and off
May have made money with their work
May know of rules/fundamentals, but cannot apply them to their own works
Can copy okay - it can get close but it still looks "off"
Make works that look decent but they do not captivate
Does not apply intention - if you ask why they did something they will say "I don't know"
Lack consistency
Cannot troubleshoot by themselves - looks to others for troubleshooting
May or may not not have a unique developed style


### Intermediates/Transitionals - Conscious Competence
> “I am learning what I need to know”
Spend a large amount of time drawing - probably daily or multiple times a week
Have most likely made money with their work
Follow the rules well, and can apply it to their own works
Can copy very well
Can make solid work from imagination that look right
May not have intention in each decision they make
Have consistency
Can troubleshoot the fundamentals
May or may not not have unique a developed style
Can give feedback but some may not be actionable enough to have meaningful change


### Advanced/Experts - Unconscious Competence
> "What I need to know comes naturally"
Spend most of their week on drawing/painting 
Working artist, heavy probability either sold works or worked for large companies and have made a career from it
Fundamentals are intuitive
Break the rules + experiments - this is where you start getting things that look unique
Give intention and meaning to everything
Bring their own experiences and voice to their works
Have recognisable hallmarks in their work - they have a recognisable and unmistakable style
Can troubleshoot everything
Can give specific and targetted feedback for most things


I am trying to look at myself clearly and honestly. As badly as I wont to consider myself "higher up" as an intermediate, I cannot truthfully say I fit past a beginner. The whole idea that an intermediate is "higher up" than a beginner is an unhelpful way to think about it and is the thing that might turn you into someone who treats people with less experience and skill as lesser worth.

But here is the kicker: you can still make appealing works and gain a following/make money if you are around the trainee - intermediate level. I see so many artists who are around the Trainee level who make appealing art and have followings, and make money. I myself have sold art as a Trainee level. This isn't about how much you or other people should value your work. Because that is something else entirely.

This is if you feel stuck, and are not sure what is holding you back. This list of fundamentals is meant to help you look at what these arem and look at your own work, and honestly ask yourself if you undertand and apply each of these.


## Fundamentals
The following list is moreso for myself to map out what I may be missing if the thing later is too difficult for me and I feel I don't know what is going wrong. I wouldn't say you need to become an expert in each thing before moving on to the next, but I would say you need to spend some time in each of them, understand what they are for and be able to say when you don't understand, and be able to pinpoint what you don't understand. I would say in these you need to have some competency in the step before to have less trouble in the step before - for example, you can't make the shapes you want if you can't mechanically draw a line the way you want, and you can't really match a value study silhouette if you haven't got competency in re-drawing a shape.

These are so basic that they go under the radar when looking at courses. Most courses do not cover these at all and assume you have these down.
You will also find that you take each step to the steps after.


### Consistency + attention
If you are not drawing regularly, you cannot understand what issues you run into so you cannot form the questions you need answers to.
If you are not paying attention when you draw, you won't be able to see when it does not look "right" - it doesn't look how you intend.
If you do not have an intention when you draw, you don't have a goal or target so you cannot compare where you are to where you want to go.
you have to pay time and attention to be able to understand concepts and be able to ask specific questions to find answers 

### Markmaking/linework
if you are doing what people call chicken scratching or your lines aren't confident (wobbly, shaky, without intention) you are mechanically not practised enough and should focus on doing straight lines and curves for a while, probably for warmups too

### Shape + proportion
If you are copying something from a reference and it looks off, chances are you are not practised in shape and proportion.
Tasks:
Mirror shape
Silhouette objects, from simple to complex

### Form + Perspective
If your drawings look flat, this is what you should focus your attention on.
This is where you solidify your intuition on the 3D shape of an object. This is where you draw boxes and cylinders and blobs and other things in 3d, starting from simple (a box and a cylinder) to complex (a vehicle or an animal).
I had it in my brain for a long time that perspective was only good for if you want to draw backgrounds, which I wasn't interested in for a long time. But in reality, perspective applied to any form will make it look less flat.

### Value + Light
If your shaded work seems to not make sense even with good form, this is where you should focus your attention.
This is nothing to do with color at this point. It is about how much the light affects the objects in your work.
You need to do value studies here - first ONLY 2 shades with no blending in Black and white. 
Then add one more shade of grey on the shadows. And then one more shade in the light to make 4 shades total.

## Important topics that seem unrelated but maybe the things blocking you
I thought this would be useful to someone, because once I discovered these, it opened my eyes to the stuff stopping me from progressing. They may not be relevant to you at all. They may fit you to a T. The following is based on my own experience. 

I am not trained in ANY mental health counselling and should not be used as a counsellor, guide or teacher on these. 

I have seen a credited psychologist, read books by credited psychotherapists, and watched videos by a credited psychiatrist about mental health to help me sort through these problems. I also journal daily, which came from The Artist's Way by Julia Cameron.

### Self Esteem
This is what you should work on if you consistently think or say "I hate my art"
I always thought my self esteem was *fine*. I, however, could not accept compliments, while being about to give them out endlessly. I also had issues accepting support, while I would go to the ends of earth to support others. I would give others the benefit of the doubt while internally beating myself up for making a mistake.

One day I decided to look up the definition of "esteem" because I never went to dissect the meaning of "self esteem" before. This is what it read:
> to regard highly or favorably; regard with respect or admiration.

This shook me. I realised I did not regard myself favourable, I did not respect myself and I did not admire myself. 

The second definition
> to consider as of a certain value or of a certain type; regard.

I hardly ever decided I was worth anything unless I got approval from another person. I wrote a bit about worth earlier

### Confidence
This is where you should focus if you have trouble posting online, applying for work opportunities
I have had self confidence issues since I was young. I was highly anxious about a lot of things - and so I didn't have the confidence to try things. I actually recall moments where I had tried to do things and had confidence, but I mainly recall being shut down in a lot of those times.

The default state of a human is to have confidence to try. Various things in people's upbringings like caregivers or peers or even strangers can so a lot to teach you to subdue this confidence. But the truth is that is the default state - a baby can't learn to walk if it doesn't have the confidence to try.
